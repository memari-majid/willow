## What the paper does

This is a PRISMA-ScR-conformant scoping review that synthesizes 62
verified-DOI sources retrieved from a multi-database search
(PubMed/MEDLINE, PsycINFO via Ovid, and Scopus, with citation-context
verification through Scite at full-text screening) at the intersection
of four literatures that *JMIR Mental Health* has long served as the
natural venue for:

1. **Just-in-time adaptive interventions (JITAIs) and micro-randomized
   trials (MRTs)** in mobile health biostatistics.
2. **Conversational AI and chatbots for mental health** — the
   peer-reviewed RCT base across Woebot, Wysa, and the recent Therabot
   *NEJM AI* trial.
3. **The digital therapeutic alliance (DTA)** — including the 2021
   *JMIR Mental Health* special issue and the 2025 Malouin-Lachance et
   al. integrative review.
4. **AI safety, ethics, equity, and crisis-handoff** in
   chat-substrate mental health deployments.

The synthesis defines five operational integration criteria (C1–C5) for
an integrated, adaptive check-in workflow and finds that **no
peer-reviewed system jointly satisfies all five**, despite each
component being separately well-developed. A subsystem-by-subsystem
feasibility analysis demonstrates that the integration is tractable
today using existing open methods, instruments, and models. The paper
proposes a *Decide-first* prototype evaluated by an MRT against a
fixed-cadence baseline as the immediate next research step.

## Why now

Three developments in 2024–2025 motivate the timing:

1. **Therabot's preregistered *NEJM AI* RCT** (Heinz et al., March
   2025) is the first published evidence that a generative-AI chatbot
   can produce large effect sizes (Cohen *d* 0.79–0.90) on MDD, GAD,
   and clinically high-risk feeding/eating disorder symptoms — but it
   uses a fixed delivery cadence rather than a learned schedule.
2. **Aguilera et al.'s DIAMANTE RCT** (2024) is the first large RCT to
   compare an adaptive RL-driven schedule against a randomly timed
   baseline in a depression-relevant population — but is
   message-based, not conversational.
3. **Nahum-Shani et al.'s qualitative systematic review** (2025)
   confirms at the field level that JITAI deployment for clinical
   mental-health outcomes specifically remains thin.

Together these three sources triangulate the gap this scoping review
maps: between the JITAI/MRT methodological maturity in mHealth
biostatistics on one side and the conversational-agent maturity in
*JMIR Mental Health*'s own pages on the other, *no peer-reviewed
deployment integrates them*. We argue this gap is no longer a
methodological problem; it is an integration-and-trial problem.

## Originality and lack of duplicate submission

This manuscript reports original work and is not under consideration
or in press at any other venue. The paper has not been previously
published in print or electronic format and has not been posted to a
preprint server. The candidate implementation referenced in the
manuscript (Willow) is a privately maintained research system under
active development by the author and is referenced for
illustrative-architectural purposes only; the prototype, the
micro-randomized trial, and any clinical or behavioral outcomes are
explicitly positioned as *future* work, not as present contributions.

There is no overlap with previously published material beyond the
standard reuse of citations, framework names (JITAI, MRT, BCT
Taxonomy, etc.), and methodological vocabulary; we have flagged any
sources that overlap with prior author publications in the
manuscript's relevant section.
